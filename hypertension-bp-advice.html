Home Blood Pressure Monitoring & Hypertension Management

This guidance is produced in accordance with NICE Guideline NG136 (Hypertension in Adults, 2019, updated 2023) and the British and Irish Hypertension Society (BIHS) standards. It is intended to support patients at Kinsealy Medical Centre in monitoring their blood pressure accurately at home and understanding when further assessment is needed.

How to Take Your Blood Pressure at Home

ℹ️
Use a Validated Upper-Arm Monitor

NICE NG136 recommends using a validated upper-arm electronic blood pressure monitor for home monitoring. Wrist monitors and finger monitors are not recommended — they are less accurate and are not accepted for clinical decision-making. See the validated devices section below for recommended brands available in Ireland.

Before You Take Your Reading — Preparation

  • Sit quietly for 5 minutes before taking a reading. Do not measure straight after walking in from outside or climbing stairs.
  • Avoid caffeine, smoking, or exercise for at least 30 minutes beforehand — all can temporarily raise your blood pressure.
  • Sit in a chair with your back supported, feet flat on the floor, and legs uncrossed. Rest your arm on a flat surface at roughly heart height.
  • Make sure your bladder is empty before measuring — a full bladder can add 10–15 mmHg to your reading.
  • Stay quiet and still throughout the reading — do not talk, check your phone, or watch TV.
  • Roll up or remove any tight clothing from your upper arm. Never take a reading through a thick sleeve.

Applying the Cuff Correctly

  • Sit with your arm resting on a table, palm facing upwards. The cuff should sit on your bare upper arm, roughly 2 cm above the elbow crease.
  • The cuff should be snug but not tight — you should be able to slide two fingers underneath.
  • Most monitors have an artery marker on the cuff — align this with the inside of your arm over the brachial artery (the inner crease of your elbow).
  • On your first session, take readings from both arms and use whichever gives the higher reading going forward. If there is a consistent difference of more than 15 mmHg between arms, tell your GP.

Taking the Reading — NICE HBPM Protocol

  • Take two readings, one minute apart, each session. Discard the first and record the second — or if your monitor averages them, use that value.
  • Take readings twice daily: once in the morning (before medication and before breakfast) and once in the evening.
  • Do this for at least 4 days, ideally 7 consecutive days, to get a reliable average for your GP.
  • Record the date, time, and reading in a diary or the monitor's app. Do not round readings — write down exactly what the monitor shows.
  • Ignore the first day of readings when calculating your average — the first day can be artificially high due to anxiety.
  • Bring your monitor or diary record to your next appointment so the practice can review your readings.
⚠️
White-Coat Effect

Many people have higher blood pressure when measured in a clinic setting — this is called the white-coat effect. Home monitoring avoids this and gives your GP a much more accurate picture of your true blood pressure. However, home monitoring alone cannot replace a 24-hour ambulatory monitor (ABPM) in all cases — see below for when an ABPM is recommended.

⚠️
Check Your Cuff Size

Using the wrong cuff size is one of the most common causes of inaccurate home readings. Measure your mid-arm circumference (halfway between your elbow and shoulder). Most standard cuffs fit 22–32 cm; large cuffs fit up to 42 cm. If in doubt, ask at the pharmacy before purchasing. Using a cuff that is too small will give falsely high readings.

Diagnosis & Staging (NICE NG136)

Clinic BP Thresholds for Diagnosis

Stage Clinic Systolic Clinic Diastolic ABPM / HBPM Confirmation Status
Normal < 130 mmHg < 85 mmHg — Normal
High-Normal 130–139 mmHg 85–89 mmHg Consider ABPM / HBPM Monitor
Stage 1 Hypertension 140–159 mmHg 90–99 mmHg ABPM/HBPM ≥ 135/85 mmHg Stage 1
Stage 2 Hypertension 160–179 mmHg 100–109 mmHg ABPM/HBPM ≥ 150/95 mmHg Stage 2
Stage 3 / Severe ≥ 180 mmHg ≥ 110 mmHg Treat without delay — no ABPM needed Urgent

ABPM Daytime Averages for Diagnosis

When interpreting ABPM results, use daytime average readings (08:00–22:00). NICE thresholds are:

  • Stage 1 Hypertension: daytime average ≥ 135/85 mmHg
  • Stage 2 Hypertension: daytime average ≥ 150/95 mmHg
  • Nocturnal dip: a non-dipping pattern (<10% fall at night) increases cardiovascular risk — document when present.

⚠️
Not at Target?

If your blood pressure remains above your target despite lifestyle changes and/or medication, your GP may arrange a 24-hour Ambulatory Blood Pressure Monitor (ABPM). This gives a much more accurate picture of your blood pressure throughout the day and night and helps guide any changes to your treatment. See the booking section below.

Lifestyle Advice (NICE-Endorsed)

Diet & Salt Intake

Try to reduce your salt intake to less than 6g per day (roughly 1 teaspoon). This is one of the most effective things you can do — it can lower your systolic blood pressure by 5–10 mmHg on its own. Aim for a diet rich in fruit, vegetables, and low-fat dairy, and cut back on saturated fat and processed foods.

Physical Activity

Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate exercise per week — for example brisk walking, cycling, or swimming. You do not need to do this all at once; 30 minutes on five days a week is ideal. Regular activity can lower your systolic BP by up to 8 mmHg.

Alcohol

Keep your alcohol intake to no more than 14 units per week, spread over at least three days, and avoid binge drinking. Drinking heavily is a common and reversible cause of raised blood pressure.

Smoking & Weight

If you smoke, stopping is one of the most important steps you can take for your heart health — smoking significantly increases your risk of heart attack and stroke alongside high blood pressure. If you are overweight, even losing 5–10 kg can reduce your systolic BP by up to 10 mmHg. Ask your GP about weight management support if needed.

Caffeine & Stress

Large amounts of caffeine can temporarily raise your blood pressure — consider cutting back if you drink a lot of tea, coffee, or energy drinks. Getting enough sleep and managing stress (through relaxation, mindfulness, or gentle exercise) also helps, though results vary between individuals.

When to Seek Emergency Help

🚨
Call 112 / 999 or Go Straight to Your Nearest Emergency Department (ED) Immediately If You Have a Very High Reading AND Any of the Following Symptoms

If your home monitor shows a reading of 180/120 mmHg or higher and you have any of these symptoms, do not wait — call 112 or 999 or get someone to drive you to the Emergency Department straight away:

  • Sudden severe headache unlike any you have had before
  • Chest pain or tightness
  • Sudden blurred vision or loss of vision
  • Sudden confusion, difficulty speaking, or weakness/numbness in your face, arm, or leg (possible stroke)
  • Shortness of breath or difficulty breathing
  • Nausea or vomiting alongside a very high reading
  • Seizure (fit)
⚠️
Very High Reading Without Symptoms — Contact the Practice Urgently the Same Day

If your home monitor shows 180/120 mmHg or higher but you feel well and have none of the symptoms above, do not ignore it. Phone the practice the same day to speak to a GP or nurse. Do not simply re-check the reading and wait — a blood pressure this high needs urgent review, even without symptoms, and your medication may need to be started or adjusted that day.

⚠️
Consistently High Readings at Home — Book an Appointment

If your home readings are consistently 140/90 mmHg or above over several days, contact the practice to arrange a review. Your GP may recommend a 24-hour blood pressure monitor (ABPM) or a medication review. Do not wait for your next routine check-up if your readings are persistently elevated.

24-Hour ABPM — Booking

✅
The Most Accurate Way to Check Your Blood Pressure

A 24-hour monitor gives your GP a much more complete picture of your blood pressure than a single clinic reading. It records your BP throughout the day and overnight while you go about your normal routine — removing the anxiety of a clinic appointment and revealing patterns that a one-off reading can miss.

Your GP May Recommend an ABPM If…

  • Your blood pressure was high-normal or elevated at a recent appointment and needs confirming
  • Your readings are not reaching your target despite medication and lifestyle changes
  • Your BP is higher in the clinic than at home (white-coat effect) and needs clarifying
  • Your BP is normal in clinic but your home readings are higher (masked hypertension)
  • Your GP wants to review how your medication is working over a full 24-hour period
  • You have symptoms that may be related to blood pressure changes overnight

What to Expect on the Day

  • The monitor is worn on the upper arm for 24 hours, typically fitted in clinic and returned the following day.
  • The cuff inflates automatically — usually every 30 minutes during the day (07:00–22:00) and every 60 minutes at night (22:00–07:00).
  • Each time the cuff inflates, stop what you are doing, keep your arm still, and breathe normally until it deflates.
  • Carry on with your normal daily routine — the whole point is to capture what your blood pressure is like in real life.
  • You can shower — just keep the small monitor unit dry and do not submerge it in water.
  • Fill in the diary provided, noting your sleep and wake times, meals, any exercise, and any symptoms you notice during the recording period.
  • A GP or nurse will review your results and contact you to discuss the findings and whether any changes to your care are needed.

Book Your 24-Hour Blood Pressure Monitor

If your GP has recommended a 24-hour monitor, please contact the practice to arrange an appointment to have it fitted. Before the monitor is fitted you will be asked to read and sign a short consent form.
