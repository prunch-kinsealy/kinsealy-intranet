CDM / PP Finding — Identifying New Patients on Socrates

▼
👤 All Staff🔄 Last updated: 05/06/2026📅 Monthly / at new patient registration
Use the steps below to identify patients who should be on the CDM or Prevention Programme but are not yet enrolled. Run this search at least monthly and whenever a new patient is registered with a CDM-qualifying condition.
1
Socrates — Disease Register
Open the Disease Register / Patient Query
In Socrates, go to Reports → Disease Register. Select the relevant CDM condition(s) to query: Diabetes (Type 1 / Type 2), Hypertension, COPD, Asthma, Cardiovascular Disease, CKD, Atrial Fibrillation, Heart Failure, Stroke / TIA.
2
Filter — Not Enrolled or Overdue
Filter for Unenrolled or Lapsed Patients
Apply filters to show patients who:
  • Have a relevant coded diagnosis but are not currently enrolled on a CDM or PP programme
  • Have not had a CDM review in the last 12 months (CDM patients) or 24 months (PP patients)
  • Were recently registered and flagged for CDM enrolment at the new patient screen (Step 3 — AI Medication Screen)
3
Socrates — Enrol Patient
Enrol in the Correct Programme
For each identified patient, open their chart and:
  • Go to the CDM tab in Socrates → add the relevant condition(s) to the disease register
  • Select the correct programme: CDM (diabetes, COPD, asthma, CVD, CKD, AF, heart failure, stroke/TIA) or Prevention Programme (PP) (cardiovascular risk, pre-diabetes, lifestyle risk factors)
  • Set the enrolment date and initial review due date
  • Document in chart notes: "Enrolled on CDM/PP — [condition] — review due [date]"
4
Secretary / Reception
Contact Patient & Send Pre-Appointment Form
Once enrolled, contact the patient to arrange their first CDM/PP review:
  • Text or email the relevant pre-appointment form via Socrates: CDM Pre-Appointment Form ↗ or Prevention Programme Form ↗
  • Do not book an appointment until the completed form has been returned
  • Add a Socrates reminder task: "CDM/PP form sent [date] — awaiting return"
⚠️ Tip — opportunistic finding: When reviewing hospital letters, discharge summaries, or specialist correspondence, always check for a newly diagnosed CDM-qualifying condition. If found, enrol the patient immediately and flag to the PM for claiming.

AliveCor — KardiaMobile (Rhythm Screening)

▼
👤 Dr F. O'Reilly🔄 Last updated: 08/05/2026📌 v1.0
Purpose
Quick single-lead ECG screening for AF and rhythm abnormalities. Used opportunistically during CDM visits or on clinical request. No formal booking required — takes approximately 30 seconds.
NS
Booking / Setup
No Formal Booking Required
AliveCor is used opportunistically — no dedicated appointment needed:
  • Used routinely as part of CDM visits (phlebotomist or nurse)
  • Can be requested by a doctor at any consultation
  • Device should be charged and paired with the Kardia app before clinic
  • 🎥 Video guide: KardiaMobile Tutorial ↗
NS
Nurse / Phlebotomist
During the Recording
  • Patient sits comfortably, relaxed — no talking during recording
  • Place both thumbs (or index fingers) on the two electrodes on the device
  • Hold still for 30 seconds — app will confirm when complete
  • Alternatively: place device directly on bare chest for a chest lead recording
  • If result is "Too Noisy": ensure dry hands, reposition, try again
  • Save trace and result to Socrates patient chart
DR
Doctor
Interpretation & Follow-Up
  • Normal Sinus Rhythm (NSR) — document, no further action required
  • Possible AF — arrange full 12-lead ECG; consider Holter monitor if paroxysmal suspected; review clinically
  • Unclassified — review trace clinically; consider full ECG if any concern
  • Too Noisy / Inconclusive — repeat attempt or arrange full ECG
  • Document interpretation in chart. If abnormal, task appropriately.

ECG — ProMed (12-Lead)

▼
👤 Dr F. O'Reilly🔄 Last updated: 08/05/2026📌 v1.0
Purpose
12-lead ECG for rhythm assessment, ischaemia screening, and baseline cardiac evaluation. Ordered as part of CDM, on doctor request, or when AliveCor yields an abnormal result.
MS
Secretary
Booking
  • ECG is booked as part of a CDM visit, or separately on doctor request
  • Allow 15–20 minutes for the appointment
  • Advise patient: no lotions or oils on chest or limbs on the day; loose-fitting clothing preferred
  • Book with nurse or phlebotomist
NS
Nurse / Phlebotomist
During the Procedure
  • Patient lies flat and relaxed — explain each step to reduce anxiety
  • Expose chest, wrists, and ankles. Clean and dry the skin — no lotions
  • Apply electrodes in correct positions:
Limb leads: RA (right wrist), LA (left wrist), RL (right ankle — ground), LL (left ankle)
Chest leads:
V1 — Right sternal border, 4th intercostal space
V2 — Left sternal border, 4th intercostal space
V3 — Between V2 and V4
V4 — Midclavicular line, 5th intercostal space
V5 — Anterior axillary line, level with V4
V6 — Mid-axillary line, level with V4 & V5
  • Ask patient to lie still and breathe normally — run 10-second trace
  • Check trace quality: no artefact, all leads present
  • Save trace to Socrates. If clearly abnormal, alert doctor immediately
  • 🎥 Video guide: [Add ECG lead placement tutorial link]
DR
Doctor
Interpretation & Follow-Up
Systematic review: rate → rhythm → axis → intervals (PR, QRS, QTc) → ST changes → T-wave morphology → comparison with previous if available.
  • Normal trace — document, no action
  • AF / flutter / heart block — urgent review, consider cardiology referral
  • ST elevation — emergency management protocol
  • ST depression / T-wave changes — clinical context, consider stress test or cardiology referral
  • Long QTc — review medications, consider cardiology
  • Document interpretation and any action taken in chart

ABPM — ProMed Tubeless (24-Hour BP Monitor)

▼
👤 Dr F. O'Reilly🔄 Last updated: 08/05/2026📌 v1.0
Purpose
24-hour ambulatory BP monitoring gives a far more accurate picture of blood pressure than a single surgery reading. It captures daytime, night-time, and average readings and is essential for diagnosing hypertension, white coat effect, and masked hypertension.
⚠️ Written consent required before fitting
The ProMed tubeless ABPM is an expensive device taken home by the patient. Consent — including acknowledgement of care responsibilities and replacement cost — must be completed in the CDM Pre-Appointment Form before the device is fitted. Do not fit without confirmed consent on file.
MS
Secretary
Booking
  • ABPM is booked as part of a CDM visit, or separately on doctor request
  • Morning appointment preferred — patient wears device for 24 hours and returns it the following morning
  • Confirm patient has completed the CDM Pre-Appointment Form ↗ and consented to ABPM before appointment
  • Advise patient: wear a loose-fitting short-sleeved top on the day; avoid tight-fitting sleeves
NS
Nurse / Phlebotomist
Fitting the Device
Before fitting, confirm consent is documented in Socrates:
  • Fit the ProMed tubeless cuff to the non-dominant upper arm
  • Cuff should be snug but not tight — two fingers should fit underneath
  • Programme device: readings every 30 minutes during the day (07:00–23:00) and every 60 minutes at night
  • Give patient the verbal and written care instructions:
    • Keep the device dry — no baths, showers, or swimming
    • Sleep with arm flat, avoid lying on the cuffed arm if possible
    • Avoid vigorous exercise during readings — keep arm still when device inflates
    • Do not remove or adjust the device between readings
    • If the arm becomes very uncomfortable, contact the practice
  • Device return: patient to return device next morning or as agreed
  • Advise patient: if device is lost or damaged, a replacement cost will apply — as per current ProMed pricing
  • Document fitting in Socrates: "ABPM fitted [date]. Return expected [date]."
  • 🎥 Video guide: [Add ProMed ABPM fitting tutorial link]
NS
Nurse / Phlebotomist
Device Return & Download
  • Receive returned device, check for damage
  • Download 24-hour trace using ProMed software
  • Save report to Socrates patient chart
  • Task doctor: "ABPM report available for review"
  • If device not returned within agreed timeframe: contact patient; escalate to PM if not returned within 1 week
DR
Doctor
Interpretation & Follow-Up
Review full 24-hour report alongside bloods and CDM context:
Target thresholds:
24-hour average: <130/80 mmHg
Daytime average: <135/85 mmHg
Night-time average: <120/70 mmHg
  • Within targets — document, no medication change
  • Above targets — review medications, lifestyle advice, consider up-titration
  • Non-dipping pattern (night BP does not fall >10%) — clinical significance; consider secondary causes, cardiology input if indicated
  • White coat hypertension (surgery BP high, ABPM normal) — reassure, monitor, avoid over-treating
  • Masked hypertension (surgery BP normal, ABPM elevated) — treat as per guidelines
  • Document interpretation and management plan in chart

Roaccutane® (Isotretinoin)

▼
👤 Dr F. O'Reilly📅 Active from: 08/05/2026🔄 Last updated: 08/05/2026
About Roaccutane®
Roaccutane® (isotretinoin) is a vitamin A derivative used for severe acne that has not responded adequately to topical treatments and oral antibiotics. It is the most effective treatment available for acne and in many cases produces long-term remission. Due to its teratogenicity and potential side effects, it requires careful patient selection, pre-treatment counselling, signed consent, ongoing blood monitoring, and monthly follow-up throughout the course.
⚠️ Roaccutane® is strictly contraindicated in pregnancy. It causes serious foetal malformations. All female patients of childbearing potential must comply with the HPRA Pregnancy Prevention Programme before, during, and after treatment. No prescription may be issued without a completed and doctor-reviewed consent form.
📋 Medical Secretaries and Doctors — please familiarise yourself with the Roaccutane® Consent Form ↗. All clinical information — eligibility, blood monitoring, side effects, contraception requirements, and pregnancy prevention — is contained within the form.

Prescribing Protocol
DR
Doctor
Dosing & Escalation
Starting dose:
10mg on alternate days × 2 weeks — reduces risk of initial acne flare and allows patient to adjust.

Escalation:
10mg daily × 2 weeks → increase slowly toward 0.5 mg/kg/day, then up to 1 mg/kg/day based on clinical response and tolerability. Reassess at each monthly consultation.

Target cumulative dose: 120–150 mg/kg total. This determines treatment duration and maximises likelihood of long-term remission. Example: a 70 kg patient at 1 mg/kg/day (70 mg/day) requires approximately 4–5 months at full dose to reach target.

Initial flare: Warn patient that acne may temporarily worsen in weeks 1–4. If severe flare occurs, consider a short course of prednisolone (e.g. 30 mg daily, tapered over 2–4 weeks).

Post-treatment: On completion, prescribe a topical retinoid (e.g. adapalene 0.1% gel or tretinoin 0.025–0.05% cream) to prevent relapse.

Corticosteroid Joint Injections — SOP (KMC-SOP-JI-01)

▼
👤 Dr F. O'Reilly (sole practitioner) 🔄 Last updated: 09/05/2026 📌 v1.0 — Review: May 2027
Scope: This procedure is performed exclusively by Dr Fran O'Reilly. No other practitioner at the practice is authorised to administer joint injections without documented competency training and approval by the practice principal.
Booking — Secretary
Patients may be booked directly for a joint injection appointment (without a prior GP consultation) if both of the following criteria are met:
✅ Direct booking criteria (both must apply):
1. It has been at least 6 months since the patient's last injection to that joint (check clinical notes)
2. It is the same joint as previously injected
⚠️ If either criterion is not met — or if this is a first injection or a new joint — book a GP consultation first. Do not book directly for the injection.
Script — Secretary / Doctor
MS
Secretary
Check patient type & task for script
GMS patients (medical card / GP visit card): Patient must bring their own Depo-Medrone steroid script to the appointment. Task Dr O'Reilly or any doctor on duty to issue the script in advance — task wording: "Joint injection [joint] booked [date] — please issue Depo-Medrone 40mg/mL 1mL script for patient to bring."

Private patients: No script required from patient — medication supplied by the practice. Advise patient to check with their insurer regarding cover and to bring their insurance policy / membership number.
DR
Doctor (Dr O'Reilly or doctor on duty)
Issue steroid script (GMS only)
Issue prescription for Depo-Medrone (methylprednisolone acetate) 40 mg/mL × 1 mL. Document in notes. Patient brings script on the day — do not administer without it.
DR
Doctor (Dr O'Reilly)
Complete consent form & proceed
Patient completes the Joint Injection Consent Form ↗ before the procedure (can be sent in advance or completed in clinic). Verify 6-month interval and same joint before proceeding.
Indications
Shoulder: Adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder), subacromial bursitis, rotator cuff tendinopathy, AC joint OA
Knee: Osteoarthritis, prepatellar/infrapatellar bursitis, pes anserine bursitis, meniscal irritation
Soft tissue: Trigger finger, de Quervain's tenosynovitis, lateral/medial epicondylitis (at clinical discretion)

Injection is not first-line — offer after conservative management (physio, NSAIDs, rest) has failed or is contraindicated.
Contraindications
⛔ Absolute — do not proceed:
Suspected septic arthritis or overlying skin infection · Prosthetic joint at site · Suspected fracture / haemarthrosis requiring orthopaedic review · Patient refusal / unable to consent · Known hypersensitivity to corticosteroid or local anaesthetic
⚠️ Relative — assess individually:
Anticoagulants (warfarin/DOAC) — apply pressure ≥ 5 min post-injection, document · Poorly controlled DM — warn of transient BGL rise 24–72 hrs, advise increased monitoring · Immunosuppression — discuss risk/benefit · < 3 months since last injection to same site (max 3/year) · Pregnancy — seek specialist advice · Complete tendon/ligament rupture suspected
Equipment
Medication: Depo-Medrone (methylprednisolone acetate) 40 mg/mL — 40 mg (1 mL) large joints / 20 mg (0.5 mL) small joints · Lidocaine 1% — 1–2 mL
Sharps: 5 or 10 mL Luer-lock syringe · Drawing-up needle (21G) · Injection needle: 21G 38 mm (knee/shoulder) or 23G 25 mm (smaller joints)
Asepsis: Chlorhexidine 2% / IPA 70% · Sterile gloves · Sterile gauze x3 · Hypoallergenic dressing
Safety: Sharps bin within reach throughout · Adrenaline 1:1000 0.5 mL IM immediately accessible in treatment room
Pre-Procedure Checklist
☐ GP consultation done — diagnosis confirmed, injection agreed
☐ Indications confirmed; contraindications screened and documented
☐ Informed consent obtained and recorded (risks, benefits, alternatives discussed)
☐ Injection to same site not within last 3 months
☐ Diabetic patient counselled — increased BGL monitoring 48–72 hrs
☐ Anticoagulated patient: agent and last dose documented; pressure plan in place
☐ Treatment room set up — all equipment on clean surface/trolley
☐ Anaphylaxis kit checked and within reach
☐ Patient positioned comfortably with injection site exposed
Consent — must document
Discuss and record in notes: Benefits (pain relief, ROM, onset 3–7 days, duration up to 6 months) · Risks (infection <1:10,000; post-injection flare 24–48 hrs; skin depigmentation/fat atrophy; tendon weakening with repeated injections; transient BGL rise in diabetics; facial flushing) · Alternatives (physio, NSAIDs, activity mod, orthopaedic referral) · No guarantee of benefit

Document: "Risks, benefits, and alternatives discussed. Patient consents to proceed."
Procedure — ANTT throughout
1
Doctor
Hand hygiene
Wash hands / alcohol gel ≥ 20 seconds before setting up.
2
Doctor
Prepare equipment (aseptic technique)
Open sterile items onto clean surface. Draw up corticosteroid + local anaesthetic. Attach injection needle.
3
Doctor
Position patient
Shoulder: Seated or supine, arm relaxed at side or across lap
Knee: Supine with knee slightly flexed (pillow beneath) or seated at 90°
4
Doctor
Identify & mark landmark
Shoulder (subacromial): 1 cm inferior + 1 cm medial to posterolateral corner of acromion
Shoulder (glenohumeral/capsule): 2 cm inferior + 2 cm medial to posterolateral acromion
Knee: Superolateral — 1 cm superior + 1 cm lateral to superior pole of patella; direct needle inferomedially into suprapatellar pouch
5
Doctor
Don sterile gloves → clean skin
Chlorhexidine/IPA wipe in concentric circles. Allow to dry ≥ 30 seconds. Do not re-palpate cleaned area without sterile gloves.
6
Doctor
Administer injection
Warn patient ("sharp scratch"). Insert needle smoothly at correct landmark. Aspirate briefly — if blood, withdraw, discard, prepare new syringe before re-attempting. Inject slowly — resistance suggests mislacement (peri-tendinous/intra-ligamentous); reposition before proceeding.
7
Doctor
Withdraw → dispose → dress
Apply gentle pressure with sterile gauze. Do not recap needle — dispose directly into sharps bin. Apply hypoallergenic dressing.
8
Doctor
Observe 10 minutes
Patient remains seated. Monitor for vasovagal episode or anaphylaxis. Then document (see below).
Patient Aftercare Instructions
Rest injected joint 24–48 hrs; avoid strenuous activity 2 weeks · Post-injection flare expected in first 24–48 hrs — manage with ice pack (15–20 min, wrapped, 3–4x/day) + paracetamol/ibuprofen if not contraindicated · Don't wet site for 24 hrs · Benefit onset typically 3–7 days; full effect up to 2 weeks · Diabetics: increased BGL monitoring 48–72 hrs · Red flags: increasing redness, warmth, swelling, fever → urgent review · No benefit after 4 weeks → reassess
Documentation — record in Socrates
☐ Date and time · Indication and clinical findings · Consent documented · Contraindications screened/excluded · Drug, dose, volume, batch number and expiry · Site and approach · Any procedural complications · 10-min post-injection observations and patient condition on discharge · Aftercare given · Follow-up plan · Clinician: Dr Fran O'Reilly
Adverse Event Management
Anaphylaxis: Call 999 · Adrenaline 0.5 mg IM (0.5 mL of 1:1000) mid-outer thigh · Lay flat, legs elevated (unless resp distress) · O₂ 15 L/min via non-rebreathe · Antihistamine + IV hydrocortisone if available · Complete incident report
Vasovagal: Lay flat, elevate legs, monitor pulse/BP, reassure. Do not re-attempt on same day. Document.
Septic arthritis (>48 hrs): Same-day urgent review. Aspirate for culture if effusion. Urgent ortho referral. Empirical antibiotics. Incident report.
Post-injection flare: Reassure — common, self-limiting. Ice, analgesia, rest. If >5 days, review to exclude infection.
Blood on aspiration: Withdraw, discard syringe, apply pressure, prepare new syringe. If significant haematoma → abandon for the day.

All adverse events: document in patient record and notify practice principal. Serious events → State Claims Agency / CISI.
Frequency Limits
Maximum 3 injections per joint per year · Minimum 3 months between injections at same site · No benefit after 2 injections → consider orthopaedic / rheumatology referral rather than further injection · Document cumulative count at each site in clinical notes

Appointments — Booking & Triage

▼
👤 Dr F. O'Reilly🔄 Last updated: 07/05/2026
Purpose
To offer every patient the right appointment with the right clinician — and to protect appointment availability for those who genuinely need it. This means establishing the reason for every visit before booking, checking whether a structured pathway already exists, and redirecting appropriately where it does. Particular attention is required for GMS and DVC patients, where appointment demand is highest.
Step 1 — Red Flags: Redirect Before Booking
⚠️ You are not expected to triage. If a patient mentions any of the following during the booking process, simply suggest the appropriate action, do not book an appointment, and document what was said and what you advised.

🚨 Chest pain → ED. Severe or persistent → call 999.
🫁 Shortness of breath → ED. Severe → call 999.
🩸 Active or significant bleeding / wound needing sutures → ED.
🧠 Suspected stroke (face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty) → call 999 immediately.
😰 Anaphylaxis / severe allergic reaction → call 999 immediately.
🤕 Head injury with loss of consciousness → ED.
🌡️ Child with high fever and rash → ED.
🔪 Severe abdominal pain → ED.
🆘 Mental health crisis / active self-harm → Pieta House 116 123. If immediate risk → ED.
Step 2 — Establish the Reason for the Visit
Always ask: "Can you briefly tell me the reason for your visit today?"
If the patient declines → document "Patient declined" and use your judgement. The doctor will address this directly during the consultation — particularly where the presenting reason appears inappropriate.
The reason for the visit should trigger one of the steps below before any booking is confirmed.
Step 3 — GMS / DVC Patients & Redirects
GMS patients account for the highest volume of appointment requests and require particular attention at the booking stage. The reason for the visit must be established before booking. If an alternative pathway — a form, a hospital query, a pharmacy — can resolve the issue without a GP appointment, offer that first.

CDM check: Before booking any GMS patient into a routine slot, check whether a CDM review is due. If so, do not book a standard appointment — route them through the CDM programme pathway instead. A routine slot must not be used to conduct a CDM review.

Do not book — redirect instead:
🏥 Hospital results not ordered by us: Patient contacts the hospital team directly. If they cannot get through, advise them to contact the hospital's PALS service. Do not book a GP slot as a workaround.
📄 Form or letter only (no clinical need): Gather as much information as possible and check whether another pathway can handle it. A charge applies — advise the patient before confirming.
💊 Prescription request only (no clinical need): Redirect to the prescription request process. Do not book a slot.
🦷 Dental: Redirect to their own dentist. If unavailable, the Dental Hospital offers a walk-in service — details on their website. Please note we are not indemnified to treat dental problems.
🩹 Dressings / wound reviews: PHN or Bon Secours wound care clinic — no referral needed. Suture/stitch removal still done here — except facial sutures placed by Plastics, which must be removed by Plastics.
🩸 Cuts or wounds needing sutures: ED or urgent care.
Step 4 — Check Patient Journeys
Before confirming any booking, check whether a structured pathway exists. If it does, follow it — do not book into a standard slot.
🔁 CDM / Chronic Disease — form required before booking: CDM Pre-Appointment Form ↗ / Prevention Programme Form ↗
💊 Contraception — age rules, booking codes, consent form
🤰 Antenatal — urine sample reminder, Combined Care form
💉 Baby Vaccines — consent form, age rules
🔬 Cervical Smear — CervicalCheck registration, consent form
🫁 Asthma — registration pathway
📱 AliveCor / KardiaMobile — rhythm screening, setup steps
🫀 ECG (ProMed) — 12-lead, booking & reporting
💪 ABPM — 24-hour BP monitor, fit & collect process
💊 Roaccutane® — consent form required first, double appt, pre-paid 💉 Joint Injections — direct booking if same joint & 6+ months; GMS bring script

▼

Sick babies/children: do not turn away — squeeze into current session, advise wait.
Elderly/vulnerable: exercise patience, facilitate where possible.
Internal: if original GP can't see, check if another doctor has a free slot.

When patients complain doctors run late:
"We always endeavour to run to time. However, when appointments start late, it creates a knock-on effect for every subsequent patient. To ensure every patient gets their full allocated time, we ask everyone arrives promptly."

  • Confirm identity and review pre-consultation form with the patient.
  • Discuss destination-specific risks: vaccines, malaria, food/water, insect bites, sun/heat, altitude if relevant.
  • Review previous vaccine history — check dates and whether boosters are needed.
  • Obtain verbal consent before each vaccine — document in Socrates.
  • Administer vaccines and record batch number, site, and dose in Socrates.
  • If malaria prophylaxis indicated — prescribe or refer to GP for prescription (nurse can counsel on compliance and side effects).
